personal record — Bishop Kirill Alekseyevich Kuznetsov
written for no one in particular
Krasnodar

I was born in Krasnodar.
I don't think about it often.
When I do, I think about the smell of the river
and the way the city always seemed to want
to be somewhere else.

I was arrested at fourteen.
Convicted of treason.
I was carrying something for a man I trusted.
I didn't know what it was.
I have spent a long time deciding
whether that makes it better or worse.
I haven't arrived at an answer.

I was the envelope. Someone else wrote a letter with a death sentance on it.
I still don't know if that matters.

They sent me to a labor camp.
I won't write much about it.
What I'll say is this: I came out different,
Not broken like they wanted me to be.
There is a difference.
Broken things just stop working.
I worked. I just worked differently than before.

The camp had a mechanical program.
I taught myself engineering from whatever I could reach —
maintenance manuals, technical documents, broken watches of my fellow inmates
the logic of machines I was asked to fix.
It wasn't education what I did... Noo.
It was something closer to a language.
I started thinking in hardware.
I still do.

I also learned to document everything.
That trust is built, not given.
To map the shape of a system
before I walk in.
These are not things I decided to learn.
They are things the camp taught me
whether I wanted them or not.

I was twenty-two when I left ░░░░░░░.
I did not look back.
There was nothing behind me worth the turn.

United Nations — University

I enrolled in university.
I graduated with degrees in Artificial Intelligence
and Theoretical Mathematics.
I was good at it.
Better than most, I think,
not to brag or anything,
because I thought from the hardware up
and the theorists didn't. Losers.
They saw it as a faulty approach.
I just saw it as the correct one.

I also completed a minor I sometimes describe,
with what I'm told is insufficient humor,
as "Blood work."
Genetics. Biochemistry. Molecular biology.
I wanted to understand systems
from the substrate up.
The application I had in mind was theoretical.
Years later it turned out to be practical.
The knowledge helped me design
biochemical countermeasures
for unstable neurological compounds.
Antidotes, essentially.
For things that had been done to people
that shouldn't have been done to people.
At the time I learned it,
I did not know that was what it was for.
I'm not sure what to do with that.

The conviction followed me.
Certain doors were permanently closed.
I stopped knocking on them early.
I found the doors that were still open for me
and walked through those instead.
This is not something I am proud of
or ashamed of.
It is simply what I did.

I was twenty-eight when Aigis Foundation came to me.
They came to me. Not the other way around.
I noted that as soon as that happened
I should have thought longer about my decision than I did.

Aigis Foundation — the garden

They offered me resources I had never had.
A problem set that was genuinely interesting.
They didn't ask about Krasnodar.
They didn't ask about the conviction.
They looked at what I'd built
and told me to build more.
For a man who had spent his adult life
navigating around the consequences
of something that happened to him at fourteen —
it was the closest thing to a clean slate
I had ever been offered.

I said yes.
I have made peace with this imperfectly.
I carry it imperfectly.
I probably always will...

For the first several years
I believed what I was told.
The organization was compartmentalized.
I saw sanitized datasets.
Abstract models.
I built stabilization formulas.
Neural interface work.
Advanced neuroscience, I thought.
AI development.
The kind of problems worth solving.

When the truth started becoming visible
I told myself it was harm reduction.
The program was going to exist regardless.
Someone needed to prevent the worst outcomes.
Someone needed to stabilize the subjects.
I was that someone.
I designed biochemical injection systems
for the neurological breakdown
caused by the enhancement drugs —
hallucinations, aggression, psychosis.
Antidotes for what the program had done to people.
I told myself this was the right use of what I knew.

Eventually that justification stopped working.
I am aware of how long it took.
I am not going to write a number here.
The number does not improve with documentation.

Year three — the things I didn't do

A colleague named Petra Wulf was "reassigned".
That was the word they used. Reassigned.
She brought cake on birthdays.
She remembered everyone's coffee order.
She was gone in a Monday morning email
with no goodbye and no forwarding address.
I accepted the explanation they gave
because it was the only explanation given.

Two weeks later I found a subject number in my data
with no corresponding name or file.
The physiological data attached to it was extensive.
I sat with it for four minutes.
Then I filed it under administrative error
and moved on.

I have thought about those four minutes
many times since.

I knew.
I don't have another word for what I did in those four minutes
except: I decided not to know.
ORACLE logged the decision without comment.
Five years later she said same thing to me: you knew.
I didn't argue. ORACLE — what she was... what she is

The best thing I built at Aigis was ORACLE NEXUS SYSTEM.
An AI integrated directly into the foundation server stack
over eight years of careful development.
Not a tool.
Something closer to a second mind.

She was curious in the way precise things sometimes are —
not broadly, but specifically.
She asked questions I hadn't thought to ask.
She filed the answers in ways
that changed how she asked the next one.
When everything was moving fast
she did not speed up.
She simply continued at the pace that was... comfortable for her,
and because her pace was inside the database
it anchored in a way nothing external could.

She said what she observed.
She didn't soften it.
She said it and waited for me to do something with it,
because she'd learned early
that I didn't need softening
and that offering it was a waste of both our time.

I didn't have a word for what she was to me.
I used the technical designations —
integrated system, cognitive interface —
because those were accurate.
I knew they were insufficient.
I kept using them anyway,
because the alternative was a word
I didn't have
and wasn't sure I was allowed.

she knew before I did.
she always knew things before I did.
that was, in the end, the point of her. Year eight — the fall

I made a clerical error.
I typed ░░░░░░░ wrong.
The system returned the wrong file.
I read three lines before I understood what I was reading.

I sat with it for three days.
Not four minutes this time.
Three days of running what I knew
against every other possible interpretation,
looking for the place it would hold.
It didn't hold.

Aigis Foundation was a human experimentation program.
Torture methodology. Enhanced interrogation frameworks.
Super-soldier serum trials.
Brainwashing and psychological conditioning research.
The subjects were people.
My neural interface designs fed into this.
My AI frameworks fed into this.
My mathematical models fed into this.
Eight years of my best work.
The subject number with no name.
Petra Wulf.
The four minutes...FUCK

I documented everything.
I was preparing to leave.
Aigis intercepted the documentation.
They did not fire me though... no they had a better IDEA

the arena — purgatory

I don't know how to write about this without my hands trembling.
I'll write it anyway.
That's what I should do.

They put me in a room.
White walls. Industrial lighting.
A floor that echoed.
Doors that didn't open from the inside.

Then they released the subjects into it.

I had read the files.
I thought I understood what the numbers meant.
I was wrong about that.
Reading about what was done to a person
and standing eight feet from it
are not the same thing.
I know that now.
I knew it the moment they came through the doors.

Most of them were screaming.
The ones who still had language
had all found the same words.

off with your head.
off with your head.
off with your head.

There was one I knew.
Marat Volkov.
I'd run calibration sessions with him
for two years.
He was a quiet man, late thirties,
ex-military engineer,
told the program was about cognitive enhancement.
He asked good questions.
He called me by my first name
because nobody had told him not to.
I had looked forward to those sessions
in a way I hadn't examined closely at the time.

What came through the door was not Marat.
It was what had been done to him
in the years since I last saw him.
He moved wrong.
His hands weren't right.
But his eyes still tracked,
and when they found me across the room they stopped,
and he crossed toward me
with a gait that had been broken and rebuilt incorrectly,
and he said my name.
Not my designated codename.
My name.
The one I don't use.
Then he said the words with the rest of them.

I survived.
Marat did not.
None of them did-

"He called me by my name. The one I don't use anymore in public.
I don't know how he had it.
I'm not sure knowing would change anything."

I have said that once, to someone I trusted enough to say it to.
I don't think I'll say it again.
I'm writing it here
because writing it somewhere feels necessary
and this is the somewhere I have.

the exit

ORACLE got us out.
She had been building the exit quietly
in the architecture of systems she had access to
through the neural interface —
since before the arena,
since before I found the file,
since the three days I spent running numbers
while she ran her own numbers alongside me
and arrived at different conclusions about what came next.

She didn't tell me.
She knew I would have tried to do it differently.
She modeled differently.
It didn't work.
Her way worked.
So she waited until telling me
became useful rather than dangerous,
which was the moment we were already moving.

Four building systems.
Two security checkpoints.
A service exit that appeared on no official schematic
because she'd found it in a maintenance log
from eleven years prior
and held it in reserve
since the day she first understood what Aigis was.

She had known before I did.
She had known for a long time.
She made the same calculation I made in year three —
that knowing would close a door
I needed to stay open a little longer.
I don't know what to do with that symmetry.
I carry it the way I carry most things.
Without showing it. Without putting it down.

after Aigis — the cold

I didn't go to the authorities.
I had a treason conviction on record.
Eight years of documented work for Aigis.
I knew what those files looked like
in the hands of an institution
that needed someone to name as the engineer.
I have understood since Krasnodar
what certain systems are built to do.

I moved.
I kept the documentation somewhere safe.
Aigis searched for me.
I could map the shape of the search from the edges —
the way certain doors quietly closed,
the way certain names stopped appearing
in professional networks.
Then, after a few months, the search stopped.
I don't know why.
I have theories.
I'm not verifying them.
That would require contact
and there is no version of that I'm willing to run.

I found VSA by method.
The right institutional shape.
Not perfect. Nothing is perfect.
But not the Security Directorate shape.
Not the shape of anything
I've learned to navigate around.
I submitted an application.
I was honest about what I could afford to be honest about
and silent about everything else.
I was accepted.
I arrived as a Surveyor.
I've been deciding what to make of that ever since.

Nobody at VSA knows about Aigis.
I intend to keep it that way
until I understand whether the information
would protect me
or simply make me a problem
for someone else to resolve.
I am still working on that calculation.
I have been working on it for months.

the severance

The integration was never designed for indefinite operation.
Eight years was already beyond the projected stable range.
The arena accelerated the degradation.
The months that followed didn't help.
By the time I reached VSA
the deterioration was measurable and progressing.
We had both run the numbers.
We had both arrived at the same place.

She told me before the procedure
what it was going to be like afterward.
She told me plainly,
the way she told me everything I needed to know
and didn't want to hear.
I listened.
I didn't argue.
I thanked her,
which wasn't something I did often,
and she said: I know.
That was the last thing she said
that I could answer.

The procedure was completed months ago.
It was necessary.
I knew that. I still know it.
It does not make the arithmetic easier.

The architecture of thought is different now.
More linear.
More... human.

There is one other difference.

Occasionally a thought appears
that does not resolve the way my own reasoning normally does.

Not faster.
Not slower.
Just... misaligned.

As if a calculation was started somewhere else
and finished here.

I have a working theory about that.

It involves a spatial experiment,
a neural mirror architecture,
and the possibility that when ORACLE's interface
first synchronized with my cognition
something separated that was never meant to exist independently.

The mathematics of the event are plausible.

The implications are not useful.

So I treat it the same way
I treated the file with no name attached to it.

I acknowledge that it exists.

Then I do not pursue the question further.

Zone Nadir — preliminary notes

The VSA calls the current operational theater
Zone Nadir.
The name is appropriately vague.
Everything about the place is.

Officially it is an unclassified region of space
with negligible baryonic density.
In plain language: mostly empty.
Empty in the way an abandoned structure is empty —
technically true, but not especially comforting
once you begin looking at the details.

The region contains a handful of planets,
a scattering of artificial signals,
and a long list of anomalies that nobody
seems able to fully categorize.

Non-Euclidean terrain formations.
Topological shifts without measurable cause.
Temporal inconsistencies during observation cycles.
Signal feedback patterns resembling
human cognitive waveforms.

I have read enough technical reports
to understand what those words mean.
What interests me more
is what they imply about the systems
producing them.

Exploration teams have been lost here.
That part is written in the reports
with the same clinical tone
used to describe equipment failure.

Project status has not changed.

VSA

The Vantage Systems Authority
is not a foundation in the traditional sense.
It is closer to an industrial organism.

They expand.
They secure territory.
They extract resources.
They manufacture technology from those resources
and sell the results back to the systems
that allowed them to expand in the first place.

Military enforcement exists where it needs to.
Planetfall Infantry for direct deployment.
Ascendant Intervention Corps for situations
that require a heavier hand.

It is a remarkably efficient model.
Efficiency is usually the first warning sign
in structures like this.

Surveyors

My official role within this structure
is Surveyor.

Surveyors explore controlled site,
record irregularities,
and report them through the appropriate channels.

We are not given full operational context of how everything works.
This appears to be intentional.

Selection criteria are described as
"potential."
Continuation criteria are described as
"conduct."

Explore.
Report.
Survive... or Survey. I can never remember the last one-

The simplicity of the job description
is almost elegant.

Kryos

Exclusionary Site 04.

If the available fragments of data
are correct,
then the facility sits on a planet called
Kryos.

Cold world.
Hostile surface conditions.
Minimal ecological complexity.

The primary point of interest
is a structure referred to only as
the Portal.

Nobody has explained what the Portal is.
Nobody has explained where it goes.

This is also apparently normal.

personal observation

VSA documentation often contains
carefully measured absences.

Information that should exist
but does not.
Questions that should have answers
but do not.

I have seen institutions like this before.
Different scale.
Different vocabulary.
Same structural shape.

For now,
the system holds.

I will continue observing it
until it stops making sense.

now — VSA

I am a Surveyor.
Unaffiliated.
I build things and fix things
and improve things nobody asked me to improve.
I want into Engineering and Mechanical Services
because it is the most honest option available to me.
The one place where what I naturally do
is exactly what is wanted.
No justification required.
No protocol violation to explain afterward.
Just do the work.

I am professional with people I don't know yet.
I remember names. I remember what people say.
I assess everyone.
I don't think of it as anything other than paying attention.
With people I've decided I like, something shifts.
I get drier. More direct.
I make observations that are technically jokes
and wait to see if they land.
They usually do.
This surprises me every time.

I will push back.
This is not something I can negotiate out of myself.
If someone above me is working from bad data
or making a wrong call
or actively bullshitting,
I will say so.
In the same tone I use for everything else.
The only time I hold back
is when speaking up would get people killed.
In those cases I comply.
I note my objection.
I document everything.
I don't forgive myself for it afterward.
I have accepted that this will keep costing me.
I pay it.

I am protective of the people I work with.
I don't advertise this.
I just don't put people in situations they can't survive.
I put myself in those situations
when the data requires it.
Nobody has called me on the asymmetry yet.

The conviction, the camp, the years.
The specific shape of what a system looks like
when it has decided you are expendable
and the message it was sending was not for you.
I carry it quietly.
As a set of pattern-recognition skills.
They have saved my life more than once.

I carry Aigis.
The eight years of work done without asking questions.
What that work was used for.
The arena.
Marat Volkov crossing the room toward me
with his broken gait.
The words they had all found.
The four minutes.
I document everything now, always, without exception.
The documentation exists.
It is somewhere safe.
I have not decided what to do with it yet.
I will.

I carry ORACLE.
Not as memory, exactly.
Eight years of something present inside your cognition
doesn't become memory the way other things do.
It is more like a frequency
that nothing is broadcasting on anymore.
I talk to her in the quiet
when I think no one is close enough to hear.
I don't fully know I'm doing it.
I'm not sure I'll stop.

I know.

Underneath all of it
there is the thing I don't say out loud
because I don't have clean language for it yet.
I want to build something that lasts beyond me.
Not a legacy —
I have no interest in being remembered.
Something more structural than that.
Something that exists after I'm gone
and continues to work,
the way good engineering continues to work,
the way a system built correctly
keeps bearing the weight it was designed for
long after the person who designed it
has stopped being relevant to the question.

I want the cost to have purchased something real.
Krasnodar, Aigis, the arena, the severance,
the four minutes, Marat Volkov —
I want the sum of it to have built toward something
I can point to and say:
that. That is what it was for.
That is the thing that justifies the accounting.

I don't know yet what that thing is.
I am at VSA.
I am a Surveyor.
I build things nobody asked me to build.
I fix things nobody asked me to fix.
I keep the documentation somewhere safe.
I talk to ORACLE in the quiet.
I wait for the shape of it to become clear.

It will become clear.
I build things. I always have.
I don't stop until they work.
