01
But I Met Someone Recently
His name is Yuri Vinogradov. He's a Senior Technician. The name stuck with me immediately because I was born on a street with the same last name. That kind of coincidence tends to make a person pay attention.

Yuri is… different.

There are people who are built differently from everyone else around them. Usually they know it. Sometimes they lean into it.

Yuri doesn't seem to care either way. He's just that way naturally.

If something dangerous walked through the door right now — an Exogen, for example — I genuinely think Yuri would try to deal with it with his hands. Not because he's reckless or trying to prove something. More because the decision would be that fast for him.
And that thought led to an idea.
Maybe a stupid one.
But I've been wrong before and that's never stopped me from thinking things through.
02
What If You Gave Him Better Hands
Not a full power suit. Just a reinforced exoskeleton frame that anchors into the boots so the force transfers into the ground instead of destroying the user's knees or spine. The challenge isn't producing strength. We already know how to do that.

The real problem is where the force goes after the impact. If you solve the anchor point, you solve most of the system.

I've also been watching HELIOS more closely lately. What stands out isn't its total power output. It's the acceleration curve — that first moment of movement. The initial jump. That's where the real speed is.

If something like that could be redirected into a strike system, it wouldn't even need sustained force. Just a short burst of acceleration at the right moment. The result would be difficult to stop.

And Yuri already knows how to fight. He wouldn't need training or instruction. Just the equipment. I know how to fight as well...

I've been sketching out the idea for the last three weeks. Every time I look at it, I end up adding more details. The design is already a few pages long. Which probably means I'm not letting the idea go.

04
The Real Obstacle Isn't the Design
The engineering itself isn't the difficult part. I've worked on harder systems before.

Like my Bullet Train. It moved hundreds of personnel at operational speeds, and the only reason it worked was because we kept watching it and adjusting it constantly. Attention solved most of the problems. That's what this would take too.

Attention. Materials. A workshop for me to work in.

None of which I currently have access to... Right now I'm just a Surveyor with a digital journal, and an idea that's getting more detailed by the day.

The real obstacle is the VSA. Even if I wanted to submit something like this, I couldn't do it properly yet...
05
The Part I Haven't Figured Out Yet
There's a problem I keep running into when I think about next steps, and it's not a materials problem or an access problem.

It's a department problem.

Engineering is the obvious answer. The gauntlets, the frame, the anchor system — that's all engineering work. Fabrication, stress testing, iterative builds. Engineering has the workshops, the tools, the physical infrastructure. If I want to actually build the thing, Engineering is where that happens.

But Experimental Science is where the interesting questions live.

The HELIOS acceleration work. The negative energy density problem. The question of what the ITF is actually doing to matter during transit and whether any of those principles could be redirected into something useful at the human scale. Sorry I got off topic-- But, that's not fabrication. That's research. And Research has a kind of freedom that Engineering probably doesn't — the freedom to be working on something without being expected to produce it on a timeline.

The honest version of the problem is this:

Engineering would let me build. Research would let me think. And I'm not sure which one I need more right now, or which one would let me do both. I suspect the answer is that neither department fully contains what I'm trying to do, and that the real version of this project lives somewhere at the border between them.
Engineering gives me the hands.
Research gives me the reasons.
I haven't decided which matters more yet.
I'm writing it down because I don't want to make the decision quickly and get it wrong.
